The Incident

May 7–8, 2026 — a physical failure inside AWS's oldest region

At 7:20 PM Eastern Time on May 7, 2026, multiple chiller units failed simultaneously inside a single data hall in AWS's Northern Virginia region (availability zone use1-az4). The cooling loss triggered a thermal-safety shutdown of the affected racks. This is the physical part of the story: an air-conditioning failure took the servers offline.

28 minutes later, in San Francisco, Coinbase engineers watched their matching engine lose quorum. At 7:48 PM ET, nearly all trading on Coinbase halted. FanDuel dropped mid-broadcast during Game 2 of the Lakers–Thunder NBA Playoff series. CME market data feeds degraded. StatusGator captured 2,370 status changes across 748 services in the next seven hours — more than 150 distinct cloud services were affected.

Full cooling system capacity was not restored until ~1:50 PM Pacific Time on May 8, giving AWS an approximate 20-hour recovery arc. Coinbase's own postmortem describes the outage as "unacceptable to us."

"At 7:20 PM ET, multiple chiller units failed simultaneously in a single data hall inside AWS's us-east-1 region (availability zone use1-az4). The cooling loss triggered a thermal-safety shutdown of the affected racks." — Coinbase Engineering, May 7 2026 postmortem

The Building

Why Ashburn, Virginia carries load-bearing weight for the internet

us-east-1 is not a "region" in any abstract sense. It is a set of physical data halls concentrated in Loudoun County, Virginia. AWS launched here in 2006 as its first region — there was no us-west, no eu-central. Every AWS tutorial, every default configuration, every "Getting Started" doc pointed here. That default never went away.

Loudoun County publishes its own datacenter counts. As of 2025, the county has 199 existing datacenters with 117 more in development. Northern Virginia holds nearly 5,000 megawatts of datacenter capacity — more than twice as much as Beijing, the closest competitor. Dominion Energy has received requests for 40,000 MW of datacenter capacity as of December 2024 — roughly three times the utility's all-time peak load.

The industry didn't choose us-east-1. It inherited it. Reserved Instance pricing locks customers into the region they started in. Cross-region data transfer costs money. Service parity between regions lags. Every serious engineering team knows the concentration risk, and every serious engineering team stays.

Amazon's own SEC 10-Q filings acknowledge this. Buried in Item 1A Risk Factors is a section titled "We Face Risks Related to System Interruption and Lack of Redundancy." The paragraph underneath lists the causes: natural disasters, extreme weather, operational failures — including from energy shortages. Amazon's lawyers write this every quarter. Investors have been paid to ignore it.

"Everything fails, all the time." — Werner Vogels, AWS CTO (coined c. 2008, still displayed on his conference-swag t-shirts)

Vogels was right about the thing that fails. He was wrong about the number of times most companies have permission to fail with it. If your entire industry runs on one physical location, "everything fails" becomes a promise about your customers, not your engineering.

The Standard

What Coinbase said in its own postmortem
"Our standard for being available does not depend on whether AWS has an outage. It rests entirely on whether we have built systems resilient enough to withstand one." — Coinbase Engineering, May 7 2026 postmortem

That is a sentence public companies almost never write. It is also the correct standard. And it is a standard that requires a working alternative to us-east-1 — an alternative that has not been built.

The public planning office in Loudoun County keeps filing paperwork. Dominion Energy keeps filing load projections. The transmission grid keeps growing. And every serious engineering team in America keeps typing us-east-1 out of habit, because the economics have not changed and the alternative has not been built.

The next chiller will fail too. The paperwork is already in.
