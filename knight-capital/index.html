What Happened

The shortest path from a routine deployment to corporate death.

Knight Capital Group was one of the largest market makers on the NYSE. They handled about 17% of all trading volume. On August 1, 2012, they deployed a software update to eight production servers in preparation for the NYSE's new Retail Liquidity Program.

Seven servers got the update. One did not. That eighth server still had an old trading module called Power Peg, written in 2003 and decommissioned years earlier. The deployment reused a configuration flag that had previously controlled Power Peg. On the seven updated servers, that flag triggered the new RLP code. On the eighth server, it woke up Power Peg.

Power Peg did one thing: buy at the ask and sell at the bid. The opposite of what a market maker wants. It executed trades on 154 stocks at a rate of millions of dollars per second. By 10:00 AM, 45 minutes after market open, Knight Capital had accumulated $440 million in losses. That was more than the company's total market capitalization.

Knight secured an emergency $400 million bailout from a group of investors, giving up 73% of the company's equity. GETCO acquired what was left in December 2012. The SEC fined Knight Capital $12 million in October 2013.

Timeline

Jul 31
Knight deploys RLP code to production servers. One of eight servers does not receive the update.
9:30 AM
NYSE opens. The SMARS routing system sends orders to all eight servers. Seven execute the new RLP logic. The eighth activates Power Peg.
9:31 AM
Anomalous trading activity begins. Power Peg buys high and sells low across 154 stocks.
9:32 AM
Knight's error account starts accumulating massive positions. Internal risk systems flag the activity but do not automatically halt trading.
~10:00 AM
Knight identifies the problem and begins pulling servers offline.
10:15 AM
Power Peg is killed. Total losses: $440 million. Knight's stock drops 75% over the next two days.
Aug 6
Emergency $400M bailout closes. Knight gives up 73% equity.
Dec 2012
GETCO acquires Knight Capital. The company ceases to exist as an independent entity.
Oct 2013
SEC fines Knight Capital $12 million for violations of the Market Access Rule.
