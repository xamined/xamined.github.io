Timeline

All times Eastern, May 6, 2010.
9:30 AM
Markets open down ~1% on Greek debt fears. S&P downgraded Greece to junk a week earlier. Riots in Athens.
1:00 PM
S&P 500 already down 2.5%. VIX up 20%+. Elevated selling but nothing unusual for a broad selloff.
2:32 PM
Waddell & Reed's sell algorithm begins. 75,000 E-mini futures contracts ($4.1 billion), pegged to 9% of volume per minute. No price limit. No time limit.
2:41 PM
E-mini prices begin dropping fast. HFT firms start hot potato trading: passing contracts among themselves, absorbing nothing.
2:42 PM
Equity markets plummet. 600 additional Dow points gone in about 5 minutes. Arbitrage linkage carries the crash from futures into stocks.
2:45 PM
In 14 seconds, HFTs generate 27,000 E-mini contracts (49% of volume) but net only 200. Market makers pull their bids. Only stub quotes remain.
2:45:28
CME triggers Stop Logic Functionality. E-mini trading pauses for 5 seconds. The order book partially rebuilds.
2:47 PM
Dow bottoms at 9,869. Down 998.5 points (9.2%). Accenture trades at $0.01. P&G drops 37%. Eight S&P 500 stocks hit one cent.
3:07 PM
Recovery complete. Most of the 600-point crash reversed in 20 minutes.
4:00 PM
Dow closes at 10,520. Down 347 points (3.2%) for the day. Exchanges cancel 20,800 trades across 286 securities.

The People

Waddell & Reed was a mutual fund company in Overland Park, Kansas. They built the algorithm that sold $4.1 billion in futures contracts without a price limit. The SEC/CFTC report identified them only as "a large fundamental trader." CME Group later argued the trade was only 1.3% of daily volume and shouldn't have caused a crash by itself.

Navinder Singh Sarao was a 31-year-old trader living with his parents in Hounslow, west London. He traded E-mini futures from his childhood bedroom using a modified off-the-shelf trading platform. On May 6, he placed and canceled roughly 19,000 orders, representing 20-29% of sell-side activity. The DOJ charged him with 22 counts of fraud and market manipulation. He pleaded guilty to wire fraud and spoofing in November 2016. In January 2020, Judge Virginia Kendall sentenced him to one year of home detention, no prison time, citing his autism diagnosis (evaluated by Simon Baron-Cohen at Cambridge) and his cooperation with regulators.

Gregg Berman led the SEC investigation. The SEC/CFTC joint report took five months. The criminal case took five years. The full picture took a decade.
