What Happened

Microsoft bought GitHub for $7.5 billion. Then AI broke it.

Starting in late 2025, GitHub experienced a sustained wave of outages across its core services. Actions, Copilot, Codespaces, CodeQL, Pages, and the merge queue all went down repeatedly. In February 2026, Codespaces hit a 90% failure rate across Europe and Asia for nine straight hours. The platform logged 37 incidents in one month alone.

The root cause was AI coding agents. Tools like Cursor, Claude Code, Copilot, and Devin generate code around the clock. A single agent account can push hundreds of commits, open dozens of pull requests, and burn thousands of compute minutes in one afternoon. By April 2026, GitHub was processing 275 million commits per week, an annualized pace of 14 billion. The year before, the full-year total was 1 billion.

GitHub's CTO admitted they started planning for 10x capacity in October 2025. By February they realized they needed 30x. The gap between what they planned for and what arrived was the outage window.

In April 2026, a merge queue regression silently reverted commits across the platform. 2,800 pull requests out of 4 million merged that day were affected. Developers found code they'd shipped the previous week had vanished. No warning, no error message.

Mitchell Hashimoto, co-founder of HashiCorp and creator of Terraform, pulled his 57,000-star project Ghostty off GitHub, calling it "no longer a place for serious work."

GitHub's response: rewriting critical paths from Ruby to Go, isolating services, and bringing in AWS. Microsoft's own cloud, Azure, could not handle the load that Microsoft's own platform was generating.
